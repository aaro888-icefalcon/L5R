# Chapter 7: The Game Master


## The Role of the GM

Roleplaying games are a group activity, with one GM and one to six additional players. The GM is typically the person who invites players to the game and determines when the group has hit its size limit. If you are the GM and you don’t know any other _L5R_ RPG players nearby, you can reach out to the greater roleplaying game community in your area or online. Hobby stores, social media groups, and online play communities are all avenues for recruiting players. You can even teach yourself and your non-gamer friends how to play RPGs by purchasing the _Legend of the Five Rings Beginner Game_, which introduces the concepts and mechanics of roleplaying games through a short adventure, and comes with a set of the custom dice needed to play the game.

### Who’s in Charge Here?

Many roleplaying games rest on the general conceit that “what the game master says goes,” and _Legend of the Five Rings_ is no exception. This is mostly for expedience: the story will flow better if the players generally accept the facts that the GM is establishing. If they don’t, the game tends to bog down in arguments.

The roleplaying game social contract thus revolves around the idea that the players give the game master the benefit of the doubt on most matters of setting the scene and story. This isn’t to say that there can never be any discussion, but if the GM shoots down an idea or wants to stick to a point, the players should accept it and move on. In turn, the GM should give the players’ ideas fair consideration, and only disallow those ideas if they create implausible situations or disrupt the GM’s plans. That said, cool, exciting, or dramatic ideas should be encouraged, as long as everyone gets their turn in the spotlight. Remember, everyone (including the GM) is there to have fun.

If someone isn’t comfortable with something going on in the story, that’s another matter entirely. Players should tell the GM if something in the story is making them uncomfortable, and vice versa. It is a good idea to establish a set of signals beforehand to clearly indicate when such concerns arise. Many excellent resources about establishing and respecting boundaries in one’s roleplaying games can be found in a list of reading materials at www.FantasyFlightGames.com.

### How to start a campaign

As noted, the first step is to invite a group of players to the game. For new GMs, a range of two to five players is a recommended range—though if you’re comfortable

with it, you can always run a game for more (or fewer) players. Once you have your group, you’ll need to decide where, when, and how frequently you want to play. One player might decide to host the game at their house or apartment in the evening, or perhaps the group could make use of open gaming space at a local hobby store. Another option is to play without meeting in person on a dedicated virtual tabletop platform (VTT), in a text or voice channel, or via web forums.

After the group has been formed, the next step is to create the player characters. Usually this is done together as a group as part of the first meeting. This lets players collaborate on their character concepts and backstories, and it helps them avoid duplicating or neglecting certain roles in the game. At this stage, you should ask the group about the structure of the campaign they enjoy, the sorts of challenges they want to face, and how comfortable they are with coming into conflict with other players’ characters. This helps you weave the story and make the best use of the tools throughout this chapter.

### How to run a session

A game session is a chunk of time dedicated to playing a roleplaying game, and as the GM, you have a great deal of influence over what the players do with this time. While they likely have their own ideas about what they want to do, you are their window to the world of Rokugan, so the things you choose to present to them help to direct their activities.

##### Preparations

Running a session often requires some preparation. A few weeks, days, or hours ahead of time, the GM prepares the needed components, including the adventure (see more on **Creating Scenarios** on page 288) and the settings and NPCs within it. This can take a few minutes to hours or even days of work, and depends entirely on the complexity of the scenario and whether the GM prefers to make things up as they go along, or run sessions with a lot of advance planning. New GMs should err on the side of planning more until they figure out how comfortable they are with improvisation.

##### Getting Things Moving

Once people begin arriving, players usually want to spend some time chatting, catching up on each other’s lives, and socializing. Between this and the time it takes for everyone to settle in, GMs should plan on a thirty-minute buffer between the time they ask people to arrive and when they begin play. Once everyone is ready, the GM can politely draw their friends’ attention away from socializing and to the game at hand.

##### Running a Scene

A scene is, at its core, a period of conflict at a particular time and place. The GM describes the setting where the scene transpires, serving as the characters’ eyes, ears, and other senses. The GM also takes on the role of any NPCs included in the scene, narrating their actions and speaking their dialogue (which usually entails making up lines on the fly!).

Besides the basics like “who,” “what,” “when,” where,” and “why,” three key ingredients are needed to create the conflict in a scene: **a goal or a desire**, one or more **obstacles** to the goal, and **stakes** for success or failure. Without a concrete goal, players may flail or drift. Without real obstacles, there is no conflict— meaning no dramatic tension—and the players may become bored. Without stakes for success or failure, both the goal and the obstacle have no meaning.

The GM’s job is to zero in on scenes with all three ingredients and use the various rules of the game to adjudicate whether the characters overcome their obstacles. This, fundamentally, is what pacing is about: slowing down and zooming in on the action—that is to say, on the conflicts—and speeding up or summarizing moments in game-world time when there isn’t anything dramatic happening. For an intense moment of inner conflict, however, it makes sense to play through the moment as if it were happening in real time.

There is one major exception to this in the _Legend of the Five Rings Roleplaying Game_, which is the “slice of life” or characterization scene. These scenes could include conversations between PCs and NPCs, cultural events, or simple moments of experiencing some part of existence like a morning routine or a yearly festival. Though these scenes seem mundane, they can serve to build the world, characterize its inhabitants, and emphasize unique elements of Rokugani culture that may be

novel or interesting to your players. These scenes also help your players get immersed in the setting. After all, playing a character who is a part of the imaginary world around them, rather than a spectator, is what roleplaying games are all about.

##### Ending a Session

How do you decide when to end a session? Sometimes a good stopping point is when a major question just got answered or a challenge was overcome, so the players can leave on a note of satisfaction. At other times, it’s important to stop because the next scene is likely to be complicated, and it will be best if everyone is fresh to play through it together another day. It can also be fun to leave off on a cliffhanger (such as when the players are about to do battle or discover the identity of a dreaded foe). This leaves players looking forward to the next session. Be sure to award experience points (see page 299) and answer questions that arose during play.

To reinforce the idea of choice and consequence, it’s very helpful to write down key events and the decisions that the PCs made as each session winds down. This way, the GM can incorporate natural ramifications to the PCs’ actions into the story, which further helps reinforce player immersion in the setting. The **Campaign Sheet** (found at www.FantasyFlightGames.com) provides spaces for recording key information about each PC.

### Managing Players

In addition to running the campaign itself, a GM is usually the group’s leader and helps resolve disputes between players about the game. People play roleplaying games in order to have fun and relax, but it can be difficult to achieve that when certain players’ fun comes at the expense of other players, or when personalities and expectations between players clash.

##### Being Respectful of Each Other

Rokugan is a world with as many complexities as our own, so mature topics and themes may arise during play. Its society comes with inherent injustices, including the caste system, Rokugan’s repressive Code of Bushidō, the use of torture, and xenophobia. It’s up to the group to determine what social issues and levels of violence or sexuality they’re comfortable featuring as part of the story. Groups should change whatever makes them uncomfortable, whether that means modifying the setting or avoiding some topics. Once these expectations have been discussed, it’s everybody’s responsibility to ensure that these expectations are upheld and everyone in the group feels comfortable and welcome.

##### Dealing with Disruptions

While ideally, everyone should be interested in the ongoing story, sometimes players tune out from events or start distracting side conversations. A certain amount of this is to be expected, but if a player constantly disrupts the game, the GM may need to do something about it.

The GM should consider taking some time outside the game to speak to that player privately. Not only can the GM let the player know that their disruptions may be spoiling the game for others, they can also take the time to ask the player what the GM can do to make the game more engaging and interesting for them. Clear and honest communication where both sides can be heard resolves most disruptions, and doing so privately makes sure the player doesn’t feel shamed in front of the rest of the group.

##### Arrivals and Departures

Sometimes, a player has to drop out of the group or a new player asks to join. If the group has advance notice, the GM can make plans to have a last session focus on resolving some or all of their character’s story. If life causes this to happen without warning, however, the GM may need to come up with a reason for the character’s absence that makes sense to the ongoing events. Some groups might be comfortable with a different player picking up the retired character, depending on the needs of the story and their openness to changes in depiction.

New players should be able to begin the game with a character who has the same experience points as the rest of the characters in the group. This also applies when players make characters to replace ones who have died or retired during their adventures.

### Evoking the Legend of the Five Rings Setting

The _Legend of the Five Rings_ RPG is different compared to many other roleplaying games, not only for its unique setting elements inspired by real East Asian cultures, but also because of its thematic focus on personal drama, court intrigue, and folkloric magic and mythology. The following considerations can help GMs bring the emerald lands of Rokugan to life and showcase the unique elements of the game and setting.

##### Making Locations Memorable

From bamboo forests to sheer cliffs, the geography of Rokugan is as beautiful as it can be dangerous. Arguably, the landscape inspired an entire religion with a focus on honoring nature, and a typical Rokugani samurai will also see wonder in the natural world around them. When describing the characters’ surroundings, keep in mind all six of their senses: what they see, hear, smell, taste, feel, and sense on a spiritual level. You can tailor your description to a particular character based on their advantages and disadvantages: a samurai with a passion for sake will notice sake carts and houses first, while a samurai with an anxiety about dogs will perceive canines to be more vicious and aggressive.

Another tip is to add what could be called “texture” to your descriptions by using a few pointed specifics instead of generalities: compare the mental picture you get and emotions you feel when you think of a “forest in a valley” compared with “a rocky hillside covered in gnarled black pines, their branches all twisted to point

##### Bringing NPCs to Life

Many of the same principles used for describing locations can be applied to non-player characters as well, including giving texture to their appearance and mannerisms. What is their style of dress, hair, and facial hair? What colors, fabrics, accessories, and perfumes do they wear? Do they maintain perfect posture, do they slouch, or do they have a limp or a nervous tick? Where can they usually be found, and what do their surroundings say about their personality? What player character personality traits might cause PCs to scrutinize qualities of an NPC or see them in a more favorable light?

NPCs have another dimension to consider, which is their dialogue. GMs can always narrate an NPC’s words, but many find it extremely rewarding to speak as though they were that character. Some of the most basic ways to vary a character’s voice are to give them different emotions or levels of energy. A GM who feels comfortable portraying accents might give peasants and sailors a more freewheeling style of pronunciation, but players should steer clear of accents that invoke stereotypes or caricatures of East Asian cultures.

Beyond varying the pitch, tone, and speed of NPCs’ speech, GMs can do a lot to differentiate characters with diction, word choice, and poetic flourishes. A literary and refined character will use more polysyllabic words and complex sentence structure. For groups that speak English for their games, the GM might use words with Latin and Greek roots to characterize nobility, reserving shorter and simpler words of Anglo-Saxon origin for more practical characters. Samurai besides those of the Crab Clan are much more apt to speak in simile and metaphor, use personification, or make allusions to nature. A wealth of East Asian poetry is available in translation online and in libraries, which can inspire and delight GMs who are interested in the aesthetics and ethos of these cultures and historical periods.

##### Dramatizing Bushidō

Adhering to the tenets of Bushidō can sometimes be even more limiting than a character’s capabilities. It doesn’t matter whether the character can sneak into and out of the shed—it matters whether they believe such an action is compatible with Righteousness and Honor. The GM should keep the tenets of Bushidō at the forefront of the players’ minds by choosing a particular tenet to feature or test as part of a hurdle at least once per session. When creating the obstacles for a story, look at the lists of possible breaches and sacrifices in the **The Tenets of Bushidō**, on page 301, to see

if any of those actions might be possible avenues for overcoming or bypassing obstacles.

##### Navigating Status and Hierarchy

Rokugan’s rigid social structure is another limiting factor on the characters’ actions. The characters are usually subordinates to one or more non-player characters, to whom they are expected to show proper deference and respect. Even when characters are placed in a position of authority, they are expected to serve as role models for their own subordinates and follow certain customs to reinforce the status differentials between parties.

These cultural elements represent a significant departure from the emphasis on forthright and individualistic expression found across many contemporary real-world industrialized countries, with their reduced emphasis on class divisions and increasingly informal modes of address compared to those of ancient times. If players are interested in exploring these facets of Rokugani culture as opposed to glossing over them, the protocols and honorifics of this social dance can become additional levers for the GM to use in awarding honor and glory, or in forcing players to stake honor and glory to momentarily ignore social custom. More specifics about status and hierarchy may be found in further supplements detailing the world of Rokugan.

### Story-First Groups

For some groups, playing the game is secondary to exploring their characters’ motivations and fates, and the thrill of “what happens next?” trumps the thrill of using particular combinations of techniques to defeat enemies. If your group falls into this category, you might consider one or more of the following:

- Techniques are reduced to purely narrative effects.
- Combat-related conflicts are mediated with a single roll, such as using the rules in **One-Roll Duels** (see page 261).
- Intrigues are handled purely as roleplay, with the GM determining who prevails based only on what players say (or describe their characters to be saying).

##### Approaching the Supernatural

That monsters, spirits, and gods walk beside mortals in Rokugan is a tremendous consideration when portraying the world of _Legend of the Five Rings_. These beings are based on the folklore and mythology of real-world cultures and religions, and while that means that GMs can lean on these tales and legends to provide inspiration for their stories and game, it’s also important to avoid trivializing or being disrespectful of the source material. This can be done by researching and developing the spirits and monsters with the same level of care lavished on locations and non-player characters, as well as by portraying them as beings of power who are not to be taken lightly or ignored by the players.

Such beings might be found anywhere, but they are typically informed by the nature of their surroundings. Creatures and beings dwelling in water tend to take on aquatic or amphibious traits, while mountain spirits have more rugged and stark personalities and appearances. In Rokugan, benevolent spirits are generally beings of beauty, cleanliness, and radiance, while evil spirits embody their corruption through ugliness, filth, and darkness. Ancestor spirits retain the appearance they had in life, while lost souls may appear as flickering lights and ghostly apparitions. Many spirits have the power to shapeshift or cloak their true appearance in order to move among mortals without being detected, so one never truly knows if one is unwittingly speaking to a god or a fox-wife.

Using the supernatural in Rokugan is a way to challenge the players’ way of thinking and methodology. Supernatural phenomena tend to place constraints on the senses that mortals rely on most, since spirits cannot typically be seen, touched, heard, or felt, except by shugenja and those with the sixth sense. Supernatural beings also place constraints on player logic, because these spirits and creatures may well violate laws of nature and physics through their actions. Finally, the supernatural impedes players’ ability to problem solve, as there are limited options for dealing with the metaphysical, and PC groups may need to rely on experts or to complete research in order to understand, placate, or withstand supernatural beings and effects.

### Roleplaying the Spirits

Excepting those from Tengoku and Jigoku, spirits don’t operate on a continuum of good and evil. They act according to their nature, which is governed by its own, inscrutable rules. These creatures have no consideration for the welfare or desires of the samurai they encounter. The exception to this is ancestor spirits, who are intrinsically invested in seeing their descendants succeed and act in ways that reflect well on their ancestors.

Thus, you should strive to make an otherworldly being’s motives mysterious, as well as use doublespeak, riddles, or illusions to trick your PCs. The very same spirits who help the PCs on a lark may turn on the party for their own amusement later.

Adding supernatural elements to investigations or exploratory scenes can be especially effective at making the characters approach obstacles with fresh eyes and a sense of wonder—and usually, by the end, a new wariness about dealing with spirits and monsters.

### Offering Choices and Determining Consequences

If we were to offer GMs only one piece of advice for running Rokugan, it would come down to this: remember that Rokugan is a world of choice and consequence. The ninjō, giri, honor, glory, and status systems all exist to force characters to make choices and to track how those characters evolve over the course of a campaign. Players should have agency to make real decisions on behalf of their characters—that is to say, to choose the way their character wants to confront or resolve a situation—and their characters should experience the fallout of those choices, both personally and in the world around them, based on what is at stake (see **Raising the Stakes**, on page 292).

The GM should strive to create choice points in their campaigns by pitting two desires against one another (see also **Devising Goals** later in this section). This typically takes the form of the conflict between a character’s ninjō and giri, but it may also be a matter of a character’s needing to choose whether to stake honor or glory to accomplish a particular task. Other choice points stem from deciding which problem to tackle first, which possible solution to pursue, or who benefits or loses from the resolution of the problem.

When designing these choice points, the GM should plan for the immediate or short-term ramifications as part of the prep, but long-term consequences can be thought out during the preparation stage between sessions. Often, choices will come up organically during play without the GM’s having devised them first, so the GM will need to use their best judgment to determine what short-term ramifications make sense given the circumstances, building off of what was previously established instead of introducing completely new concepts. Honor and glory rewards (see pages 300 and 304) should also be taken into account in the resolution of choice points.

Providing choices between right and wrong, or good and evil, promotes a more heroic narrative and epic scale. Campaigns in which bad choices are pitted against worse choices foster a darker and grittier feel. The default mode for the _Legend of the Five Rings_ setting exists in a middle ground, one in which players are offered different choices whose outcomes may not always be what they seem. None of these modes is particularly better or worse than any other, but each leads the player characters down a vastly different path.

### Creating Scenarios

Earlier, we discussed how scenes require three ingredients to be worth slowing down and running in real time: **goals**, **obstacles**, and **stakes**. On a small scale, these three things form an encounter or scene in the story, one of many in a given roleplaying game session. String together a series of scenes with multiple goals and obstacles, with evolving stakes, and you have the nuts and bolts of an adventure—in writing terms, a narrative arc, or scenario.

However, roleplaying games differ from novels or movies, because the “audience” (the players) get a say regarding which goals their characters want to pursue, how their characters try to overcome those hurdles, and whether their characters can affect the stakes!

##### Devising Goals

The goal, or motivating desire, is the most basic element of narrative. In forms of media as ancient as medieval poetry and as modern as video games, goals are often framed as quests. In roleplaying games, goals might also be called quests, but in Rokugan, they are more often framed as aims. These aims are necessary because if a character doesn’t want anything—or doesn’t want to accomplish anything—then the events of the story simply happen to them. If a character moves through a story unaffected emotionally, they are static, and do not really change, for good or for ill.

### Grittier Games

If your group wants an extremely lethal experience for their PCs, you could apply one or more of the following to make the game substantially more likely to result in death and dismemberment:

- Increase the Deadliness of all weapons by +4.
- If a character has the Bleeding condition at the end of the scene, they perish unless they spend a Void point.
- The Lightly Wounded and Severely Wounded condition can fester. Each time a character fails a Fitness check to remove this condition, the conditions worsen (from Lightly Wounded to Severely Wounded, or Severely Wounded to dead).

Given that goals are so important, how does the GM come up with goals for the characters to pursue in the game? In the _L5R_ RPG, two goals are built into every character at character creation: their personal goal, or ninjō, as well as their duty to their lord, or giri.

Ninjō is fairly straightforward: the character wants this thing, and they will be pursuing it during the campaign, sometimes even through pivotal scenes.

Giri should also contain an actionable goal, and GMs can create adventures by creating obstacles that make those goals harder to achieve. Sometimes these obstacles may even be a lord acting against their own best interests, forcing a character to decide whether to disobey their lord to better serve them!

Although these goals don’t have to be mutually exclusive, some conflict between ninjō and giri can make for great roleplaying inspiration. Choosing between one’s desires and one’s duty can make for a thrilling story.

##### Erecting Hurdles

Goals wouldn’t be interesting if they weren’t difficult to achieve. Completing a goal may only require a character to do one thing, or they may need to break down their goal into many smaller tasks, or sub-objectives. Both the original goal and sub-objectives must have **hurdles**, or obstacles, that the PCs must overcome.

In RPGs, hurdles typically take one of these forms:

- **Martial Hurdle:** Do they need to fight someone or something to achieve it?
- **Social Hurdle:** Do they need to talk to someone to achieve it?
- **Investigative Hurdle:** Do they need to find out more information to achieve it?
- **Explorative Hurdle:** Do they need to go somewhere to achieve it?
- **Logistical Hurdle:** Do they need to get something to achieve it?
- **Emotional Hurdle:** Do they need to change their beliefs or personality to achieve it?

You should consider giving your players some leeway to determine how their PCs try to get what they want (choosing between types of hurdles and their approach to overcoming them). If there’s only one “correct way” to solve problems, players usually feel that their agency to exert influence over the story has been taken away. “One correct way” also goes against the spirit of the mechanics themselves, as elemental approaches showcase the idea of solving a challenge through different methods.

GMs can brainstorm a list of steps needed for PCs to achieve a goal and work backward from there, or they can also use the different types of hurdles to help them brainstorm the steps. That way, the GM can plan to have their socially focused group be met with NPC adversaries, while their martially inclined group gets to enter into combat in order to accomplish their goals. The different types of goals are explained broadly below—it’s up to the GM to translate the general ideas into specifics for their campaign depending on the exact characters, places, and objects included in their story. The GM should also remember to incorporate specific Bushidō tenets into at least one hurdle per session to keep the constraints of the warrior’s code at the forefront of the players’ concerns (see also **Dramatizing Bushidō**, on page 286).

### Tactical Groups

Tactically minded players tend to prioritize understanding and exploiting the rules of the game. They might not be as invested in the backstories of their own characters, much less those of non-player characters, and may see combat as its own end, because it’s a chance to test out a cool combination of techniques and abilities. If your group has some of these traits, consider the following alterations to the rules:

- Encourage roleplaying, but make mechanics a major focus during conflicts like intrigues.
- Make all rolls open, and tell the players the TN of all checks they make.

Social Hurdles

Social hurdles result when characters have competing interests and must be persuaded to abandon those interests or yield to the characters competing with them. Persuasion, intimidation, negotiation, and begging are the weapons wielded in these types of hurdles, with parties extracting promises or trading threats to achieve their aims. Intrigues are a more complex way to model these types of interactions in the game; otherwise, roleplaying can yield the same result if the players can figure out sufficient motivation or leverage to sway the opposing characters.

- Which NPC stat blocks will you use, and which will you need to create from scratch?

Investigative Hurdles

Investigative hurdles result from missing or withheld information. Who or what did this thing? How did they do it? Where and when? Why? For characters who have been trained in the Kitsuki Method, an investigative hurdle could entail investigating a room for physical evidence, whereas shugenja could lean on their relationships with the spirits to catch a glimpse of supernatural clues. Characters may also have to consult certain sources of written information, such as a character’s pillow book or scrolls in an exclusive library. In most cases, testimony is also required to piece together the missing details. In this way, investigative hurdles intersect social hurdles when a character has the desired information and must be persuaded to give it up.

- What information is absolutely necessary for the characters to progress to the next step? Any story-stopping hurdles should be accessible to the players without a roll.
- What details might the PCs notice right away with various vigilance scores?
- What bonus information is accessible in the scene that isn’t necessary to advance to the next hurdle? What additional context or implications does it provide? What skills can be used to unlock those extra clues?
- Are there any other entities who are following the same trail, and what will they do if they discover the PCs are also interested?
- Are the PCs collecting the information in a socially or legally permissible manner? Remember that the testimony of an honorable samurai trumps evidence in the Rokugani justice system.
- What political ramifications does the unearthing of buried information have on social harmony? How will different factions react?

Logistical Hurdles

Logistical hurdles result when the characters do not have the tool, skill, or guide they need to complete a task. It’s the physical version of the investigative hurdle. The characters may need to figure out where they can get the required item (resulting in an investigative hurdle) and then figure out how to get it. In Rokugan, common methods of acquisition include barter, payment, and even doing errands or tasks in return. Sometimes getting to the needed item is the hurdle, which can make the hurdle martial, social, or explorative as well.

Explorative Hurdles

When the setting or environment itself is the barrier, the group is met with an explorative hurdle. Maybe the area is difficult to navigate or hard to traverse due to its physical characteristics, requiring that characters be fast, strong, or agile to overcome barriers. Environments may also serve as a barrier when the characters don’t know the route to their destination and must blaze new trails to find what they are looking for. Explorative hurdles intersect with logistical hurdles if the characters need to outfit an expedition, and they can become martial hurdles if the terrain includes hostile inhabitants.

Emotional Hurdles

The last hurdle is the most difficult one to dramatize (that is to say, illustrate in the fiction of the game as opposed to explaining outright), as it takes place inside a character’s mind. In the emotional hurdle, the character must change their own behaviors in order to succeed. Perhaps they have refused to give up on a certain goal or way of thinking and have become obsessed, and so they must let go in order to move on with their life and find mental peace. Or maybe they have refused to take a certain path or try a certain activity because of their prejudices, and they must learn to let go of their bias in order to grow and improve themselves. Disadvantages are especially well suited to becoming emotional hurdles, as they are stumbling blocks the player has already singled out as things they want to engage with during play. Note, since emotional hurdles happen in a character’s mind, players and GMs may need to get in the habit of describing what their character is thinking to interact with them.

- What are some situations that might force the problematic emotion or disadvantage to become apparent?
- What types of NPCs or personalities will exacerbate (or soothe) the problematic emotion? How can they be worked into the situation?
- How will NPCs and society react to the character when they express this emotional hurdle?
- What does the character need to do in order to counteract the effects of the problematic emotion or disadvantage? Is there a way that the character can unmask to temporarily overcome the hurdle, and what are the consequences?
- If this were a movie, what setting backdrop or props might the director choose to further illustrate the emotions in the scene?

##### Raising the Stakes

The final component to crafting scenarios is the **stakes**: what will or won’t happen if the characters succeed at their goal or fail to accomplish it. Put another way, these

are the risks the character could incur as consequences to their actions, and the rewards that they might reap as a result. Risks and rewards can take the shape of various physical, social, mental/emotional, and spiritual effects. Players are great resources for coming up with potential risks and rewards, since they usually know what they want their character to get, and what would feel like a punishment. And often risks and rewards are the very things that shape a character’s desires in the first place.

GMs should remember that failure, too, must be interesting. If the characters don’t achieve their goal, the story should still proceed—there’s no “game over” screen in roleplaying games. What happens next? Certainly, the PCs might be put into a bad spot or have to sacrifice more resources in order to get what they need, but even in rare cases in which the entire group gets killed (such as in a skirmish or mass battle), the river of time still flows in Rokugan, and their deaths will have an impact on the fortunes of their lord and their families.

- Physical risks include injury, disease, and death, as well as loss of wealth or property. On the other hand, while Rokugani society considers physical rewards less impressive or important than other motivators, “getting stuff” can still be a powerful reward. Weapons and armor, equipment, property, and wealth all can serve as a reward and counterpart to physical risks.
- Social risks include embarrassment and shame, loss of position or reputation, losing friends and gaining enemies, or at its worst, exile. Social rewards include glory, titles, allies, and successful romance. Social rewards may also come with physical rewards, as status and property are often interlinked in Rokugani culture.
- Mental and emotional risks can be presented in the game by gaining new disadvantages, strife, or—more narratively—a character’s unhappiness, frustration, or impaired mental faculties. Rewards can include gaining new advantages, training in skills or techniques, or—more narratively—happiness, pride, and other positive emotions.
- Finally, Rokugan is a profoundly spiritual world. Characters can acquire karma from their actions, earn the enmity or patronage of specific Fortunes or spirits of the land, and if they traffic with foul forces, they might be afflicted with the Shadowlands Taint. Most spiritual risks and rewards can be represented in the game through certain advantages and disadvantages.

### Using Lords and Duty

In the **Game of Twenty Questions**, players are asked to work with the GM to create a lord to whom they have a sworn duty. If a group of players is brand new to Rokugan, you can use this section to design a lord for them. If the group consists of veterans of the _L5R_ setting, you may let the group create their own lords.

##### One Lord for the Whole Group

The game can be easier to run if all the characters are serving a single lord, as this means they all have the same broad objectives: those of their lord. In this style, all of the characters serve one clan, and usually a single family. In a single-lord campaign, the GM develops adventures that hinge on the fortunes of that lord and their secrets—secrets hidden from outsiders due to the potential for shame or controversy.

The single-lord campaign style highlights interpersonal connections between the characters, who are likely related through blood or marriage, have trained and served together for some time, or grew up in the same region and share memories of the same places and festivals. The GM should provide the group with a central “home base,” such as a castle, magistrate’s manor, town, or religious site; there, the characters can receive their lord’s orders, gather resources, and recuperate between excursions. GMs should encourage players to personalize this home base to make it a memorable part of the game. This can also make the base a prime target for rival forces, escalating the drama.

##### Multiple Lords for the Group

Although the single-lord game is easier on a GM, players often gravitate toward the multi-lord campaign because each player has a favorite clan they want to represent. This is by far the most common way to play _L5R_, but it requires the GM to create a context in which it makes sense for the characters to come together and stay together over the course of several scenarios. Two good ways to tie the group together are to create shared goals or circumstances. The GM can use both in tandem to glue the party together even more firmly. See **Conflicting Duties** on page 294 for more on games where the PCs might be pursuing different ends.

Shared Goals

When characters all want the same thing, they share the same goal. Because they each have their own lord’s goals to consider, this shared goal must supersede any individual lord’s objectives. In Rokugan, this is done

through the Celestial Order: If the competing lords are from the same clan, it’s the clan’s interests that take precedence. If the competing lords are from different clans, it’s the Emperor’s interests that supersede all others. In a game dealing with supernatural threats, the will of Heaven or the kami can unify the group.

Shared Circumstances

Sometimes, groups find it enjoyable to play characters who want different things and compete with each other. In these cases, it isn’t shared objectives that bind them together, but their circumstances. These circumstances may be external ones, such as an assignment from their lord or a natural disaster, that compel the characters to stay in close proximity to one another for a set amount of time. The characters appear in each other’s stories because they are so close to one another. After some time, the shared circumstances typically evolve into shared goals as the samurai from different clans work together to overcome their situation.

**CONNECTING GIRI TO A LORD**

**A lord’s retinue and servants may include any number of player characters as well as all the other non-player characters whom the lord relies on to execute their duties. These supporting characters can be a source of allies or rivals (sometimes at the same time) for the PCs. A specific task that would fall to a member of a lord’s retinue is a good example of a giri for a player character.**

### Imperial Offices and Ministers

- **Office of the Emerald Champion:** Oversees enforcement of Imperial laws.
- **Office of the Ruby Champion:** Oversees training of new Emerald Magistrates and maintains the scrolls codifying Imperial law.
- **Office of the Jade Champion:** Oversees enforcement of Imperial laws against heresy and sorcery. Currently not filled.
- **The Imperial Legions:** The Emperor’s armies, fielding warriors from all clans.
- **The Imperial Treasury:** Oversees collection of Imperial taxes.
- **The Imperial Census Bureau:** Oversees collection of demographic information.
- **The Imperial Cartographers:** Oversee archives of Imperial maps.
- **Office of the Imperial Household:** Oversees servants in the Imperial palace, as well as the Seppun Honor Guard and Hidden Guard, which are responsible for the physical and spiritual safety of the Imperial family, respectively.

Alternatively, these circumstances may come from within, such as through interpersonal bonds. One character might be a hostage or ward of another character’s family; two other characters might be married, betrothed, or in-laws; additional characters might be linked as siblings, cousins, or even adopted kin. This presents a shared goal to protect one’s family (even if family members don’t like one another, blood ties often prove strong enough to bind characters together).

##### Creating a Lord

To create a lord, the GM or players should determine their **concerns**, what **complications** might arise to make managing their concerns difficult (and cause them to rely on the PCs), their **retinue** and **servants**, and finally, their personal **agendas** and **qualities**.

A lord’s **concerns** are informed by the type of position they hold as lord and comprise the duties for which they’re responsible. This is analogous to a player character’s giri.

**Complications** are the typical things that conspire to make the lord’s duties more difficult to achieve. These usually come from external forces. For the purposes of creating scenarios, complications can be translated into hurdles (see page 289) as well as stakes (see page 292).

A lord’s **retinue** sets the resources they have available to solve their problems. The larger their retinue, the less reliant they will be on the PCs for logistical support. A lord’s personal **agenda** is the thing that they’ll pursue if left to their own devices. This is analogous to a player character’s ninjō. A lord’s personal agenda may also serve as an internal force of complication to make their job more difficult. When creating scenarios, these can be translated into goals (see page 288).

A lord’s personal **qualities** include their demeanor, passions and anxieties, and distinctions and adversities, as well as appearance: what they look like, dress like, sound like, and so on. Personal qualities can be any of the things that help them do their job well or, at times, prevent them from effectively administering their duties. Concerns, complications, and retinue can be determined by a lord’s general role (examples below). After selecting a role, decide on the lord’s personal qualities and how these fit in with their role or clash against it.

Ministers and Governors

Ministers are officials tasked with overseeing a single aspect of a castle, town, or Empire-wide bureaucracy. These lords form the basis for very narrowly focused campaigns, such as those revolving around politics, war, criminal investigation, or mysticism. Ministers include clan magistrates, castle ministers, and spiritual advisors

such as abbots and priests, as well as bureaucrats from Imperial administrations. Nomenclature varies somewhat among clans. In particular, the Unicorn Clan has absorbed organizational traditions from other cultures.

One step up in prestige from ministers are the city governors, who are appointed to oversee the administration of a city. A campaign featuring a city governor as lord allows a group to focus on the fortunes of a single location, with the occasional excursion for outside threats. Governors’ complications and concerns tend to be larger, including taxation and environmental concerns, and their retinues include various ministers in addition to lesser functionaries.

### Conflicting Duties

Whenever the GM runs a multi-lord campaign, there is some inherent conflict between the characters’ giri as each lord pursues their objectives. This can and should be embraced as part of the fun of the game. Drama arises when there is conflict—when characters struggle to choose between betraying the group to benefit their clan, or betraying the clan in favor of their newfound friends.

As the players create characters, the group should decide whether they are comfortable with this sort of drama. If not, then the GM should ensure that the characters’ giri are compatible, but for campaigns with split loyalties, there are two ways to handle intrigue between PCs: **open** and **closed**.

In the open manner, which is recommended for most groups, players have fun knowing things their characters don’t, and pushing their characters into uncomfortable positions and revelations. It can help to have out-of-character discussion about how that story unfolds just to make sure everyone is on the same page. This mode also encourages more collaborative storytelling between the players and GM.

In the closed manner, which is recommended only for experienced groups, players may pass notes to the GM or send messages in between sessions to describe what their character is up to. Part of the game becomes trying to unravel the other players’ secrets. Trust is key for this mode, and it’s important to discuss boundaries and expectations beforehand, such as whether it’s okay for characters to try to kill, steal from, or disgrace other characters.

**ONE LORD, MYRIAD SAMURAI**

_Despite the need for secrecy, it isn’t unheard of for a lord to have members of other families from the same clan in their service if those samurai’s area of expertise is outside the purview of the lord’s family’s specialty. Because samurai from the same clan have pledged fealty to the clan champion, they are honor bound to work together to further the interests of the clan._

- **Concerns:** Executing the responsibilities such as upholding laws and norms, overseeing rites and ceremonies, investigating lawbreakers and heretics, overseeing development projects, and ensuring stability and productivity.
- **Complications:** Expectations from superiors, unclear divisions of power (who is ultimately responsible for a particular thing?), unclear chains of command (who outranks whom?), rivals for promotions, rivals of intent, scandals and cover-ups, lack of resources, and changing laws.

Provincial and Family Daimyō

Provincial daimyō make up the backbone of the Empire’s feudal system and are the lowest ranking daimyō. They oversee a province, a collection of cities, towns, and the land between them. Campaigns featuring provincial lords focus on a single area but encompass a wider variety of locations, including castles, towns, villages, rural shrines, and wilderness locations.

Each province is known for particular cultural and specialty items, from festivals to foods and arts to geographical features. GMs should determine the primary means by which the province feeds itself, whether farming, fishing, hunting and gathering, or even imports. Most provinces border three to four other provinces, but those in the far-flung regions of the Empire may be relatively isolated, with only one or two neighbors huddling on the frontier alongside them.

Unlike city governors and ministers, provincial daimyō are not appointed but inherit their titles through a predetermined line of succession. There are usually three or four provincial daimyō reporting to a single family daimyō. The family daimyō oversees the daimyō of all their provinces from their ancestral castle, which may also serve as the capital of a province.

In each clan, there is one leading family whose daimyō also serves as champion, to whom all others swear loyalty. A clan champion is technically in charge of their own province as well as overseeing the affairs of several provincial daimyō and all of their clan’s family daimyō, but in reality it simply means that their ministers do more work on the champion’s behalf. The families and clans are those listed in the **Game of Twenty Questions**, but this template can also be used to model the daimyō of vassal families and champions of Minor Clans, albeit on a smaller scale.

- **Concerns:** Collecting taxes from citizenry and governors (or subordinate daimyō, for family daimyō/clan champions), ensuring security, cultivating and developing the land, levying armies, overseeing the sensei of the clan schools (family daimyō/clan champion), cultivating heirs, and approving interfamily and interclan marriage (family daimyō/clan champion).
- **Complications:** Environmental issues and disease, interests of neighboring provincial lords, peasant revolts, competing interests of neighboring families and clans, incompetent subordinates, meddling family members, Imperial ordinances.
- **Retinue and Servants:** Most player characters who attain the rank of samurai are eligible to serve as members of the provincial daimyō’s court. Provincial daimyō can also oversee city governors, clan magistrates, and ministers.

## Running the Game

The GM is the players’ guide to Rokugan, helping their characters maneuver and act within the story. The rules of the game are tools for the GM and players to help give structure and a sense of order to these interactions. The following section provides guidance on how best to use many of these rules to enrich the narrative and keep the game running smoothly, along with some additional details for social attributes (see page 300).

### Rules Adjudication

While the GM and players are collaborating to tell a story, the GM is ultimately the arbiter of all rules matters. The GM should attempt to keep the pace moving during a game session and not get too bogged down in looking up details at the table—they can always reference the relevant sections later to clarify the correct way things should be resolved in the future.

The players should endeavor to support the GM rather than undermining them. Players should feel free to offer their interpretations of a rule when it becomes relevant, or if the GM asks, but they should let the ruling stand once the GM has made it. Discussing the rules after a session can be one way to untangle unclear issues or apparent contradictions.

### Multiple elemental Approach Options

Sometimes, multiple elemental approaches are viable for the same task. In these cases, the GM should offer the player a choice of which approach they want to use. For instance, to get support for an idea, a character could use their Fire Ring with a Social skill to incite interest in their idea, or they could use their Air Ring to trick someone into supporting it. These have very similar outcomes, at least in terms of getting support in the short term. However, the target number to succeed (hereafter referred to as the difficulty) for these two tasks can and should vary—some people are easily excited but hard to mislead, while others are calm but gullible.

If you’re letting the player choose their approach, it’s helpful to tell them which ring will make the check easier and which will make it harder, so that they can make an informed choice. Additionally, it’s a good idea to remind them that different approaches can have different consequences for success or failure, and to give them a hint of what these might be.

##### Varying Difficulty in Text

The text for a check sometimes indicates a variable difficulty by ring by including the alternative difficulties for specific rings in brackets after the check. This is expressed in the text with a description such as “a **TN 3 Fitness check (Earth 1, Fire 4)** to keep their footing on the swaying deck of the ship.” This means that the check has a TN of 4 if the character uses their Fire Ring (an aggressive and hasty approach that is disadvantageous for staying balanced), but a TN of only 1 if the character uses their Earth Ring (an approach defined by patience and caution), and a TN of 3 for any other ring.

##### Different Solutions, Different Challenges

Many problems have numerous possible solutions, so a character might be able to choose between two or more different elemental approaches for the same skill or even different skills from different skill groups to solve the problem. For instance, when attempting to get to the other side of a river (the Fitness skill, from the Martial skill group), a character might leap over it (Overwhelm, the Fire approach to a Martial skill) or swim across it (Shift, the Water approach to a Martial skill). A character might even use another skill entirely (such as Labor, to build a bridge, or Theology, to persuade the water kami to grant passage). See **Chapter 3: Skills** (page 140) for more on various methods they enable.

This does not mean that a character’s chosen method is unimportant. Even if multiple solutions exist, some are usually easier to implement than others. The TN of a task can vary based on the skill and approach determined by the character’s intended results and method. If a character is walking into a task with only cursory information, they might not know which approach will be the hardest until the GM announces the TN. If the character has foreknowledge of the task, on the other hand, the GM might want to hint—or even state outright—that an approach is easier or harder.

If a skill or one of its approaches is ill-suited to a situation, the GM should increase the TN of the check by 1–3. If a skill or one of its approaches is particularly well-suited to the circumstances, the GM should reduce the TN by 1–3 (to a minimum of 0).

### When to Reveal or Conceal the TN

Much of the drama (and strategy) of choosing which dice to keep on a check derives from how much strife a character is willing to suffer in order to succeed, as well as whether they are able to activate other abilities as part of the check using [opportunity] symbols. If the player knows the TN they are trying to achieve, they will have the agency to choose interesting dice combinations.

In contrast, if the player does not know the TN, the choice of which dice to keep has a “push your luck” incentive to avoid accidentally keeping too few [success] and [explosive success] symbols. While this can heighten tension at the table, many players will favor taking as many [success] as possible to reduce the risk of accidentally failing, ignoring chances to use [opportunity] to enhance the narrative.

For the majority of checks, we recommend the GM tell the players the TN. However, there are some rare occasions when the difficulty of a task would not be something the characters could accurately assess. Hidden TNs emphasize the mystery of a check, and can thus be good for showing that the PCs are out of their depth, such as when dealing with very powerful foes, supernatural beings, or unforeseen circumstances

To compensate for the loss of available information, after a character performs a check with a hidden TN, the character gains 1 Void point.

### Using Bonus Successes for Extra Effect

Sometimes, a check might yield specific additional results for bonus successes. For instance, many Attack action checks deal more damage if the character has bonus successes. The GM can put this to use on any check, allowing a character to achieve the goal more thoroughly with bonus successes.

For instance, if a check to look for subtle tells in someone’s countenance is a **TN 3 Sentiment (Air) check**, the GM might inform the player that they can get additional details with two bonus success. The GM can inform the player of this before or after they choose kept dice, but remember that informing them beforehand makes their choice much more interesting.

### Applying Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages and disadvantages can add a great deal of flavor to a scene. Conflicts in samurai stories are often determined by the slightest differences between combatants, or one warrior’s unique qualities. The GM and players should make full use of these descriptive traits at appropriate moments, shaping the story based on their characters’ individual qualities. The GM can record each character’s advantages and disadvantages on the **Campaign Sheet** for ease of reference.

##### When to Skip Advantages and Disadvantages during a Check

While advantages and disadvantages can be applied to any check for which they make sense, a good rule of thumb is to use them only when it might be decisive. For instance, if a character has already failed, there is no good reason to apply their disadvantage unless the amount by which they fall short matters. Similarly, if a character is already successful, it might not be worth spending much time applying advantages unless bonus successes or removing strife are very important.

##### Assigning New Advantages and Disadvantages

While each character starts with advantages and disadvantages, characters can also end up acquiring more in the course of play (see **Gaining Advantages and Disadvantages**, on page 99). Critical strikes can permanently wound characters, leaving them with a scar disadvantage, while the Shadowlands Taint can infect a character’s very soul. Honor and glory can give characters advantages that represent their good reputation and personal convictions (or infamy and doubts).

A character’s social circumstances can be represented with advantages and disadvantages. A powerful alliance might earn a character the Support of [One Group] distinction. In contrast, spurning a group or coming into conflict with them might cause a character to be assigned the Scorn of [One Group] adversity. These advantages and disadvantages should generally take significant effort on the part of the players to obtain.

### Using [opportunity] in Play

Opportunity gives GMs and players a chance to add narrative flair to their checks and activate additional abilities. However, it can also create a feeling that one needs to examine every possible option for [opportunity] on a check. It can be a lot of pressure to be creative on the spot—but it doesn’t need to be.

Remember that the default options from **Table 8–1: Spending [opportunity]** (see page 328) are always available. Assisting another character on a similar subsequent check (for [opportunity] if the first character fails their check or [opportunity] [opportunity] if the first character succeeds on their check), for instance, is always useful. More importantly, it can make for a simple but effective narrative moment of two characters working in concert to overcome a challenge before them that fits in nearly any context. Maybe one character calls out a warning to the other, letting them cut down a foe they wouldn’t have seen. Perhaps it is as simple as the two exchanging a reassuring glance before going into a challenging negotiation. While long descriptions can bog down play, even a short aside about how a character is helping a comrade can enrich the story by underscoring the bond (or tensions) between them. These little moments can help to reinforce their relationship, and they are sometimes more memorable in the long run than even the more narratively pivotal scenes when taken as a whole. A player or the GM can even poll the group for ideas for using [opportunity], asking if anyone else has an idea for how to spend it, narratively or mechanically. Sometimes, the GM or another player will have a good idea already in mind.

Above all, whether you’re a player or GM, try not to stress out over [opportunity]. If you can think of a good use for [opportunity] from one of your abilities or a relevant table, use it. If you have a great, creative idea to use [opportunity], pitch it to the group. And if you can’t think of anything, use one of the basic options from **Table 8–1: Spending [opportunity]** (see page 328) or let it go unused (in the case of minion NPCs especially).

### Adding Complications

Ninjō and giri inform the players and GM about a character’s relationship with their desires and their duty. In many works of fiction about samurai, a character strives to maintain their personal values while achieving glory in the eyes of their lord. During this process, the character’s perspectives on honor and glory are often challenged, and these perspectives frequently evolve as the character develops. Alternatively, a conflict between one’s personal beliefs and the needs of a sworn lord might drive a character’s development over the course of a story.

The twin motivations of ninjō (a character’s internal feelings, tied to their belief in Bushidō) and giri (the oaths to their lord that they must uphold to advance in the world) are levers that the GM can use to create interesting, dramatic confrontations between characters, or even within a single character. Complications, as described on page 39, are a way that both GMs and players can pull these motives into the story. GMs can choose to use the rules for **discord** to create conflicting complications for the PCs.

##### Discord

Discord functions in a simple manner: choose one PC’s giri and another’s ninjō, then simultaneously introduce complications for each that set the two motivations at odds with each other through the use of a pair of complications introduced at the same time. The discord tracks allows the GM to determine these in a randomized manner for a more procedural approach, but planning in advance is equally valid.

##### Building the Discord Tracks

Once all characters have chosen their ninjō and giri, the GM should fill in the discord tracks on the Campaign Sheet (found at www.FantasyFlightGames.com). One track is for giri, and the other is for ninjō. The GM writes each character’s name outside of the tracks as indicated, and then each character’s giri on the inner track and ninjō on the outer track.

##### Choosing a Session’s Discord

For the first session, the GM should begin at Spoke 1 for both tracks. For future sessions, the GM should begin at the spokes that contributed to the last session’s discord for each of the two tracks.

To determine a session’s discord, roll and keep a number of [ring die] equal to the number of players (resolving [explosive success] as usual). Total the number of [success] and [explosive success], and then count that many spokes counterclockwise on the giri track, skipping any blank spaces. The final spoke indicates the character whose giri is part of the session’s discord. The GM should start thinking about a complication they can introduce that stems from this PC’s giri.

Next, total the number of [strife] and [opportunity], and then count that many spokes clockwise on the ninjō track, skipping any blank spaces. The final spoke indicates the character whose ninjō is part of the session’s discord. The GM should start thinking about a complication related to this players’ ninjō, and how it might clash with the prior complication.

The GM may inform the player whether it was their character’s giri or ninjō that was rolled if they want to let the player start thinking about ways to work it into the story—or not, if that builds tension more effectively.

### Awarding XP

Character growth is an important theme of _Legend of the Five Rings_, as each character pursues mastery within their domain while expanding their knowledge of the wider world. The recommended rate of experience point gain is 1 XP per hour of play, with an additional 3–5 XP awarded to each character in the group at the GM’s discretion whenever the party overcomes a significant milestone or obstacle. Increasing the base value to 2 XP per hour will make for a more heroic-feeling game, and is often a good idea to help keep tactically oriented groups engaged.

GMs should feel free to adjust these numbers as they see fit. At their discretion, GMs can also give bonus XP for particularly strong moments of roleplaying among the group. Standardized XP across the group makes it easier for everyone to track and helps keep the characters at a roughly equivalent level of competency, but GMs should also feel free to award XP on a per-player basis if that is their group’s preference.

### Character Death and Retirement

Player characters in _Legend of the Five Rings_ live three feet from death at all times, so one or more PCs might well meet their end during the story. Alternatively, a character might be retired from play because they have completed their duty, because they have been stripped of their status as a samurai, or because they have simply become too old for such adventures and wish to spend their final years in contemplation at a monastery. It is recommended that GMs allow a player whose character has perished or otherwise been retired to create a new PC with the same amount of experience as their late character. The possibility of character death can be an important part of _Legend of the Five Rings_, but it should always be an occurrence that serves to show the stakes of the narrative and makes the story more interesting rather than a “punishment” for a player’s decisions.

Additionally, the GM should keep in mind that death is often the least interesting way a character’s story can end. If the PCs are vanquished in battle, for instance, instead of simply dying, they might be taken hostage (hostages are valuable, after all) and need to escape. If a PC falls into a river from an incredible height, they could die—or they could awaken downstream, gravely injured but alive. Even a lethal blow from a critical strike can often be healed to stabilize a character before the Dying condition causes them to expire, and if no PC is trained in Medicine, an NPC might be able to step in to save their life (likely with an expectation of a significant favor down the road, of course). Sometimes, the dice decide that a PC’s time is up, but frequently, the GM has some leeway to interpret the results as best serves the story.

## Using Social Attributes

Honor, glory, and status are important aspects of a character in _Legend of the Five Rings_. Not only can knowing these values for their characters help players roleplay these individuals more aptly, but the GM can also use honor, glory, and status as tools to help players engage with the setting. The PCs are immersed Rokugani even if the players are not. Honor, glory, and status exist to help bridge that gap, giving players clear indicators of costs their choices might incur and the standing of others in the world.

It usually falls to the GM to bring these attributes into the game, although players can certainly make use of them actively as well. Reminding a player that they must choose to have their character forfeit 1 honor if they wish to make that snide remark to someone of higher status reinforces the importance of courtesy in Rokugan. Similarly, noting that a character must stake glory if they wish to boast that they will be the first into the enemy’s castle the next day can be an excellent way to help bring the society the characters live in to the forefront of the players’ minds. Knowing one’s status relative to that of another character is often vital to properly interacting with them.

Players can make checks to determine an NPC’s honor, glory, or status to predict their behavior (see **The Art of Investigation** on page 170). The GM can have NPCs do the same to the PCs, and should have them give different reactions based on these values and what the NPCs know about the PCs.

When a player chooses to forfeit or stake their character’s honor or glory, consult **Table 7–1: Honor and Glory Forfeits/Awards** and the relevant section of this chapter for guidance on how much honor they must give up to perform the deed.

### Honor in Play

Literally meaning “way of the warrior” in Japanese, Bushidō is the code by which nearly all samurai in Rokugan strive to live. Samurai are taught to reach for these ideals at the expense of their own personal gain. Of course, samurai are also human, so the “breaking point” when one abandons Bushidō out of self-interest or the need for self-preservation makes for rich story and roleplaying material. The other rich story opportunity associated with Bushidō occurs when its tenets inevitably come into conflict with each other. What does a samurai do when their lord commands them to do something dishonorable? What does a samurai do when Honesty clashes with Courtesy? When Justice calls for blood but Compassion counsels mercy?

Honor is a code of behavior meant to guide the members of the samurai caste, but it does not align exactly in all places with Rokugan’s complex legal system, which imposes different standards upon people at the different levels of society. Some things are dishonorable but not considered crimes for samurai—killing someone of lower station without just cause might be within a samurai’s legal rights (provided that person is not considered another samurai’s charge), but it is still a severe violation of the Bushidō tenet of Compassion (Jin), which dictates that a samurai should have regard for even these lives. Almost all criminal activities are on some level dishonorable, though occasionally, breaking the law is less dishonorable than the alternative.

**Table 7–2: Honor Scores** offers concrete examples

of what a character’s honor attribute says about their moral fiber. As detailed in that table, if a character’s honor enters a certain range, the character receives one or more **virtue**-type distinctions or **flaw**-type adversities. These are chosen by the player, based on the parts of the code their character holds dearest or spurns. These are removed if the character’s honor rises or falls outside of the specified range..

##### Forfeiting and Staking Honor

Characters must forfeit honor to breach the tenets of their code, and are awarded honor when they make sacrifices in the name of these tenets. Because honor is internal to the character, this occurs no matter how few witnesses there are to their fateful decision.

##### Awarding Honor for Deeds

Honor cannot be gained without adversity. As such, the GM should award honor to characters for deeds that put their code above their immediate needs or desires. Characters generally should not be awarded honor for things that cost them nothing or are not accompanied by risks. A trifling breach or sacrifice is any that the GM deems too small to be minor but too large to fully ignore.

**BUSHIDŌ IN HISTORICAL JAPAN**

_Rokugani Bushidō is quite different from anything that existed historically in Japan. While historical samurai had deeply held convictions that they lived and died for, there is no single text that defined Bushidō for all samurai._

_Outside of Japan, cultural understanding of Bushidō comes in large part from retrospective efforts to understand the past. Inazo Nitobe’s_ **Bushido: The Soul of Japan** _(1900) is one text written by a Japanese scholar to explain Bushidō to English speakers. Nitobe offers “Military-Knight-Ways” as a translation of Bushidō, and expands it to the “‘Precepts of Knighthood,’ the noblesse oblige of the warrior class.”_

_Much as the code of chivalry in Europe has been romanticized to fit with the values of later writers, Nitobe’s account of Bushidō is a product of its own time (after the era of samurai), and it can help readers understand the history of Western views on Bushidō._

### The Tenets of Bushidō

Each of the seven tenets listed below is accompanied by a number of examples of situations when a character might have to forfeit honor to disobey it, need to stake honor on upholding it, or receive an honor reward for giving something up to pursue it. The GM can use these as guidelines for honor losses and awards.

##### Compassion (Jin)

Just as the farmer does not grow crops merely to fill their own belly, the warrior does not fight for just their own sake. A samurai must be constantly aware of their duty to protect others.

Breaches of Compassion

Example breaches of Compassion include:

- _(Minor)_ Verbal cruelty to a foe or stranger, allowing easily prevented suffering, tormenting animals
- _(Major)_ Verbal cruelty to a friend, physically harming someone for revenge alone, letting an ally die without trying to save them
- _(Massive)_ Murdering someone of lower status in cold blood, physically harming someone for fun

Sacrifices for Compassion

Example sacrifices for Compassion include:

- _(Minor)_ Giving someone a common useful item, spending two or more downtime scenes helping a friend, treating a foe’s wounds after a battle
- _(Major)_ Giving up your chance to be awarded 6 or more glory for someone else, saving someone’s life despite knowing they may trouble you later
- _(Massive)_ Giving away an irreplaceable item, completing a dangerous heroic task for someone else who cannot repay you

##### Courage (Yū)

Only fear of death can destroy life; the samurai replaces it with an understanding of danger. Common folk allow fear to swallow their hearts. An extraordinary soul is one with the courage to live as if each moment were the last.

Breaches of Courage

Example breaches of Courage include:

- _(Minor)_ Allowing fear for yourself to influence your actions, leaving a lost battle to protect your lord

### The Clans’ Views of Bushidō

Each clan has one or more tenets it views as paramount, even above the others. When a character chooses to forfeit honor due to defying one of their own clan’s paramount tenets, they double the amount of honor they must forfeit. When a character is awarded honor as a result of making a personal sacrifice for this tenet, the GM should double the honor they are awarded.

- **Crab:** Courage
- **Crane:** Courtesy
- **Dragon:** Sincerity
- **Lion:** Honor
- **Phoenix:** Righteousness
- **Scorpion:** Duty and Loyalty
- **Unicorn:** Compassion

Each clan has one or more tenets of Bushidō it views as less significant than the others. When a character chooses to forfeit honor due to defy this tenet, halve the amount of honor they must forfeit (rounded up, to a minimum of 1). When a character is awarded honor for making a personal sacrifice in the name of this tenet, halve the honor they are awarded (rounded up, to a minimum of 1).

- **Crab:** Courtesy
- **Crane:** Courage
- **Dragon:** Duty and Loyalty
- **Lion:** Compassion
- **Phoenix:** Sincerity
- **Scorpion:** Honor and Righteousness
- **Unicorn:** Courtesy

_Within the fictional_  
_world of Rokugan,_

_honor is believed to be more objective than it is subjective. The Rokugani code of honor does vary somewhat in interpretation, but samurai mostly agree on what the perfect samurai should believe, live for, and die for. It is a harsh code, and it values some lives above others._

_Unlike in historical Japan, the bulk of Rokugani_

- _(Massive)_ Preventing your lord ‘s major or massive breach, suffering a severity 8 or higher critical strike for your lord

##### Courtesy (Rei)

A samurai is neither a bully nor a brute killer. Samurai must treat their enemies with Courtesy.

Breaches of Courtesy

Example breaches of Courtesy include:

- _(Minor)_ Drunkenness or coarse language in the presence of someone of higher status, letting an insult to your clan or family pass without remark
- _(Major)_ Directly insulting someone of equal or higher status, letting an insult to your ancestors, sensei, or lord pass without remark
- _(Massive)_ Directly insulting your lord

Sacrifices for Courtesy

Example sacrifices for Courtesy include:

- _(Minor)_ Paying a sincere compliment to a foe, allowing a tired foe a moment’s respite, letting someone of higher status receive aid first
- _(Major)_ Giving an unarmed foe a weapon or setting aside your own weapons to fight fairly
- _(Massive)_ Taking an enemy of higher status captive without killing or maiming them

### Glory in Play

Glory is the external measure of a character’s outward success as a samurai. The higher a character’s glory, the more widely they are recognized as a paragon of samurai virtues; the lower a character’s glory, the more others believe they are unworthy of the name. See **Table 7–1: Honor and Glory Forfeits/Awards** on page 300 for how much glory a character must lose or stands to gain for a given admission of failure or acknowledgment.

##### Forfeiting Glory

A character must forfeit glory to undertake certain actions publicly. When a player chooses to forfeit or stake their character’s glory, consult **Table 7–1: Honor and Glory Forfeits/Awards** for guidance on how much glory they must give up. Unlike honor, glory relies upon external perception, so actions taken clandestinely do not require forfeiting glory, though sometimes they require staking it upon avoiding discovery. Sample diminishments for various decisions include:

- _(Minor)_ Ignoring an insult to your person, choosing to share credit for a deed, admitting a lie publicly, declining a gift from someone of higher status, losing an ancestral weapon
- _(Major)_ Murdering someone of lower status, choosing to leave the public eye for months, not arriving to a duel, attacking a surrendering foe
- _(Massive)_ Murdering someone of equal or higher status, ordering a retreat in a mass battle, refusing seppuku when required

##### Awarding Glory for Deeds

When a character is recognized for a notable deed (real or fabricated), the GM should award that character glory based on the degree of acclaim it represents, such as:

- _(Minor)_ Receiving a gift from someone of higher status, beating a foe with glory 40+ in a duel or public contest, marrying or arranging a family marriage to someone of higher glory
- _(Major)_ Commanding your side to victory in a mass battle, defeating a foe with glory 60+ in a duel or public contest, defeating an oni or similar monster with a group, marrying or arranging a family marriage to someone of higher status
- _(Massive)_ Defeating a foe with glory 80+, defeating an oni alone, winning a war, marrying or arranging a marriage into the Imperial family

**FAME**

**Glory is not exactly a character’s fame; a character who is known for deeds both good and ill might have a modest glory value due to the conflicting accounts of their actions. It does, though, confer advantages that reflect fame (or disadvantages that represent infamy) if it reaches certain highs (or lows).**

**Glory is best described as how much the outside world (with a particular emphasis on the character’s sworn lord) believes the character is a proper samurai.**

### Status in Play

Status is much more static than honor and glory: rarely do characters need to forfeit it, and generally, they only receive it as a reward upon receiving a new title. If a character does need to forfeit status, they often reset it to a specific value; for instance, if a character retires to a monastery, their status becomes 25 (a monk’s status). Rarely, characters can be stripped of status. To willfully endanger their status, a character must stake an amount determined by the GM on covering up their actions.

##### Comparing Status

Status is most often used to know which of two characters stands higher in the Celestial Order. This has many uses, both narratively and with regard to abilities. The Social skills reference interacting with characters of lower, higher, or equal status, as do many examples of times a character must forfeit (or be awarded) glory or honor.

The GM should compare the status of the PCs to that of NPCs as relevant. Most NPCs defer to those of higher status and expect obedience people of lower status— how they express this can hint at their true nature.

##### Titles

A title is assigned as a result of narrative events, but besides that, it functions much like a school curriculum: a character can spend experience to deepen their understanding of how to fulfill its functions, and eventually unlock a special ability after spending the listed XP. When a character is assigned a title, their status might change based on the Status Award listed in the title.

A title exists in parallel to a character’s school. A character can only have one incomplete title at a time. Each time a character purchases an advancement, the character may allocate the XP spent to completing their title (with full value for listed advancements and half value for non-listed advancements, as with a curriculum). XP allocated to a title does not count toward the character’s current school rank (and vice-versa).

Once a character has allocated the amount of experience listed in the title’s listed completion cost, the character acquires the title ability associated with the title. The title is then considered complete (though narratively, the character retains it), and the character may be assigned a new title as narrative events permit.

## Alternative Campaign Styles

The land of Rokugan is fertile imaginative ground in which GMs and players can grow vivid stories, and not all of these tales are focused exclusively on noble samurai torn between the sworn duty their role demands and their inner desires. From desperate rōnin and outlaws inspired by literature such as _Water Margin_ and the films of Akira Kurosawa, to groups of elite warriors protecting the whole of the Emerald Empire from supernatural threats, there are numerous other stories worth telling in the lands of the _Legend of the Five Rings_. This section describes a few alternate campaign styles you might want to explore, how to create characters for those games, and how these games can differ from our default playstyle and conventions.

### Rise from the Dust

Instead of the samurai of the Great Clans and their intrigues and struggles, this playstyle focuses on the forgotten heroes and villains who live on the outskirts of society and struggle to survive in a dangerous world. While defending a village from bandits, fleeing the consequences of one’s actions, or scraping together a few bu for one’s next meal may not be the stuff of epic poems, it is just as harrowing for those who live it.

##### Assumptions of this Playstyle

This sort of story hinges on the idea that the player characters are outsiders in the broad scope of society. They are not notable political actors as the default playstyle assumes, and they are not likely to be given any political power they do not seize with their own hands. If they are samurai, they have likely forsaken or lost their titles and become rōnin, but they may also be merchants, ashigaru, peasants, and burakumin. As a result, the company is often much rougher than the high courts of the Emerald Empire—courtesy is still important to some people, but being

discourteous is only as dangerous as the person you have chosen to insult.

##### Character Creation Alterations

Apply the following alterations to the **Game of Twenty Questions** (see page 41) for each character:

**Question 1:** _Which clan’s lands did your character_

_grow up in?_ The mechanical choice is the same, except that your status is a value of your choice from 20 to 24. After all, the peasants and samurai of a given region share many cultural values (even if most samurai are loathe to admit it).

**Question 2:** _Who is your family?_ Unless you are a fallen

samurai, your family falls far beneath the notice of the Great Clans—you might have no family but the comrades you choose for yourself. Use the Rokugani peasant family above unless you are a fallen samurai, in which case choose a family as normal.

**Question 3:** _Where did you learn their skills?_ Unless

you are a fallen samurai, use the Worldly Rōnin school on page 87. If you are a fallen samurai, use a school package as normal, but your starting honor is 30.

All characters use the following starting outfit: Traveling clothes, any 1 weapon of rarity 7 or lower, traveling pack, any 1 item of rarity 5 or lower.

**Question 5:** _What is your past?_ Instead of selecting a

giri, select a past. A past is the set of circumstances that led you to life on the fringes of society. Your past might be a grim reputation as a warrior that you seek to outrun, circumstances of birth that forced you to be a rōnin, or the enemies who pursue you.

Your past should meet the same rough criteria as giri—something that can clash with your desires, and can create drama for your character that you will enjoy during the campaign. To attain glory, you must overcome your past, but the more glory you acquire, the more likely your past is to come crashing down upon you.

**SAMURAI AS FOES**

**In this playstyle, samurai are potentially one of the largest dangers to the PCs, They might be allies during a given story, for many samurai have use of characters like the PCs. However, most samurai will be unlikely to risk their lives for the PCs, and often view them as disposable.**

### Fate of the Realm

Rokugan is a land where supernatural powers run rampant and, sometimes, provincial intrigues are truly not the greatest threat in a story. GMs and players who wish to explore stories about champions banding together to save cities, provinces, or even the world itself can certainly do so in the Emerald Empire, as the setting is rich with cosmological threats.

##### Assumptions of this Playstyle

This playstyle assumes that the player characters are samurai who are tasked with dealing with a suitably epic problem—or series of problems—that threaten multiple clans, and perhaps even the Empire itself. Dangers such as massive Shadowlands armies, bloodspeaker sects, lunar cultists, and other, more insidious threats create story opportunities for characters to undertake epic quests to protect their homes and loved ones. This playstyle mirrors more “classic” fantasy adventures of great deeds, mighty heroes, and dire foes to be vanquished.

There are numerous locations in Rokugan where this sort of campaign fits most naturally. The Shadowlands encompass a vast realm surrounding the Festering Pit of Fu Leng, and this region is the grave to countless heroes. The Shinomen Forest is an untamed wildland within the Empire itself, to which no clan lays claim and in which primal beings still stir. Even within smaller patches of wilds, there are strange and dangerous beings. Not all spirits are friendly to humans, and some see the expansion of towns into the wild lands as an affront to nature. The PCs might need to find compromise between ancient forces and humanity so that both can survive and thrive.

##### Character Creation Alterations

Apply the following alterations to the **Game of Twenty Questions** (see page 41) for each character:

**Question 5:** _What is your character’s appointed quest?_

Instead of a giri, each PC has a quest: a task given them by their lord, by their own will, by supernatural forces, or perhaps by destiny itself. This task might be to set something right in the universe, recover or destroy a magical artifact, seal a particular evil entity again for another ten thousand years, or face a particular foe who threatens the entire realm. As with a giri, a character’s quest should be able to conflict with their ninjō, and sets the means by which the character will strive for glory. At the discretion of the group and the GM, all characters might share a quest, or they might have different quests with overlapping objectives.

**Extra Experience:** After character creation, each char-  
acter receives 30 XP to spend on advancements.

##### Nemuranai

Nemuranai, meaning “unsleeping” in reference to the object’s kami, are miraculous artifacts of spiritual power. They are items that, through centuries of use, miraculous origins, or other incredible events, have attained power and even a degree of awareness. While they can appear in any campaign, they are usually vanishingly rare, accessible to PCs only under exceptional circumstances. In this campaign style, however, each PC chooses one nemuranai with an appropriate sealed invocation of their choice that they possess as their quest begins. They must also determine how the item relates to their quest:

Awakened Weapon

A weapon nemuranai has the standard profile, plus:

- Its deadliness is increased by +2, and it has the Durable and Sacred qualities.
- It has a distinguishing quirk with narrative effects.
- It has one Attack or Scheme action invocation of Rank 1–3 sealed within it. Once per game session as an action, the wielder may perform this invocation, substituting the skill used to wield the weapon for Theology.

Awakened Armor

An armor nemuranai has the standard profile, plus:

- It has physical and supernatural resistance +2, and it has the Durable and Sacred qualities.
- It has a distinguishing quirk with narrative effects.
- It has one Support or Movement action invocation of Rank 1–3 sealed within it. Once per game session as an action, the wearer may perform this invocation, substituting Fitness for Theology.

Awakened Item

An item nemuranai has the standard profile, plus:

- It has the Durable and Sacred qualities.
- It has a distinguishing quirk with narrative effects.
- It has one invocation of Rank 1–4 sealed within it. Once per game session as an action, the holder may perform this invocation, substituting Meditation (or other skill of the GM’s choice) for Theology. While wielding this item, the character adds a kept [ring die] set to an [opportunity] result to their Theology checks using sealed invocation’s Element.
