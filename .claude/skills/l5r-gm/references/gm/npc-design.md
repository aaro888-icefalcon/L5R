# Chapter 8: Non-Player Characters


Non-player characters are the other inhabitants of the world, with whom the player characters interact. In story terms, the PCs are the main protagonists, and NPCs can be thought of as the supporting cast: bit characters, friends and allies, rivals, and antagonists. Although they can be extremely important to the arc of the narrative (the call to aid from an old friend or

the diabolical scheme of a villain  
might be the sorts of events that  
initiate a story), in most scenes,  
these characters are not in focus.  
The majority of the time, the story  
that the group tells flows best if it  
is framed around the player char-  
acters and their perspectives,  
decisions, and actions.  
Like other elements of the  
world outside of the PCs, NPCs  
are controlled and portrayed  
by the GM. Portraying a wide  
variety of characters can be a  
challenge, but it can also be  
one of the best parts of sitting  
in the GM’s seat.

## NPC Profile Breakdown

GMs can, of course, invent their own NPCs, but many printed materials provide premade NPCs, along with the various rules needed to make the most of them in play. An NPC profile consists of the following elements:

- **NPC Type:** This denotes the importance of the NPC in the story. –– **Minions** are minor characters, who have simplified profiles for ease of use. –– **Adversaries** are narratively weighty characters, who have rules similar to a PC.
- **Conflict Rank:** Each NPC has a rank in two categories (combat [combat] and intrigue [intrigue]). These are estimates of the NPC’s capabilities in each of those spheres (See page 310 for details).
- **Rings:** Each NPC has a set of ring values (see **The Five Rings**, on page 32).
- **Attributes:** Each NPC has a set of attributes, just as a player character does (see **Derived Attributes**, on page 36).
- **Demeanor:** This section offers a simplified snapshot of an NPC’s personality, providing their responses to different approaches to Social skills (See page 310 for details).
- **Advantages and Disadvantages:** NPCs can be given prewritten advantages and disadvantages (see page 99), but printed NPC profiles usually have specialized advantages and disadvantages that do not appear in that chapter. These are treated as distinctions (or adversities) that apply as the GM deems appropriate.
- **Skill Ranks:** Each NPC has ranks in skill groups or skills that apply to their checks (see **Chapter 3: Skills**, on page 140).
- **Favored Weapons and Gear:** Any items the NPC is likely to have on their person, potentially including weapons they are likely to wield. Most own other possessions, but this section covers those most pertinent to their interactions with the PCs.
- **Abilities:** NPCs possess abilities, which sometimes include techniques also available to player characters and at other times consist of wholly new powers and effects. An NPC’s techniques sometimes differ from the versions available to player characters.

### NPC Types

There are two types of NPCs: minions and adversaries.

##### Minions

Minions are characters of lesser importance in the scope of the story: they might be minor characters in a town or village, or they might be foes with little dramatic weight. Because they have a smaller role in the story, they have simplified character profiles.

PCs can often engage more than one minion at a time, though some are more powerful than others. Being a minion doesn’t necessarily mean that a character is weak, simply that their expected role in the story doesn’t require more complex rules. Minions behave like other characters, with the following exceptions:

- When a minion’s fatigue exceeds its endurance, it is simply **defeated**. A defeated minion is slain if the source of damage that defeated it is 7 or higher; otherwise, it is non-lethally dispatched.
- If a minion would be dealt a critical strike, it receives fatigue equal to the severity instead.
- The game runs most smoothly if minions only spend [opportunity] on options listed in their abilities, but the GM may waive this at their discretion.

##### Adversaries

Adversaries are NPCs of substantial narrative significance. These might be rivals or powerful allies to the PCs, and they stand on the same footing as player characters. Their profiles are organized differently than a PC’s profile for the GM’s convenience, but each adversary is a match for one or more PCs in its arena of expertise. Adversaries behave like standard characters.

**NPCS ARE PEOPLE TOO**

**Remember that NPCs are more than just a set of rules and statistics!**

**They are supporting**

**characters, and can have interests, quirks, and desires on a narrative level beyond the statistics detailed in their profile.**

**Such flourishes will help make them feel realized and compelling.**

**TRACKING NPC STRIFE**

**NPCs accumulate strife in the normal manner (see page 29), but**

**the GM need only track their strife if it is actually likely to become**

**important. Otherwise, strife they generate can simply be used**

**as a general indicator for how to portray the character. If an NPC’s strife is being tracked and exceeds their**

**composure, they should generally unmask at the start of their next turn unless they are an exceptionally disciplined individual.**

**For guidance on dealing with strife with large groups of NPCs, see NPC Squads on page 317.**

### NPCs and Checks

GMs should keep the following advice in mind when rolling dice for NPCs.

**Rolling Dice:** Keep the resolution of NPC rolls

quick, using a bit of narration as needed to help the players understand what is going on.

**Spending [opportunity]:** Don’t overthink it. [opportunity] can inspire

memorable moments, but not every check needs a cinematic turnabout. If it’s taking more than a few seconds, take a look at the NPC’s profile, which often contains abilities for [opportunity]—or just let it go unspent.

### Demeanors

Each NPC profile includes a demeanor. A demeanor is a common personality type for characters of that sort, and it makes some Social skill checks easier while making others harder. Each demeanor lists an increase to the TN of Social skill checks with certain rings (“+1” or “+2”) and a decrease to the TN of Social skill checks with other rings (“–1” or “–2”), to reflect the fact that some characters are much more resistant to particular avenues than others, and prone to certain outbursts.

GMs are also encouraged to give NPCs unusual demeanors (or invent new ones, with modifiers and ways to unmask as they see fit) to keep the PCs on their toes!

##### Ambitious

This character pursues power whenever possible and is extremely resistant to giving it up. However, they jump to support appeals that line up with their own desires.

**Social Skill Check TN Modifiers:** Fire +2, Water –2  
**Common Way of Unmasking:** Bend Principles

##### Assertive

This person is used to being in charge, and does not like being told what they can or cannot to do. They are most pliant when they think they are getting their way.

**Social Skill Check TN Modifiers:** Earth +2, Air –2  
**Common Way of Unmasking:** Rage

##### Detached

This individual is extremely meditative, spiritual, enlightened, or simply uninterested in other people.

**Social Skill Check TN Modifiers:** Earth +1, Fire +1, Void –2

**Common Way of Unmasking:** Expose an Opening

##### Gruff

With a rough exterior and a no-nonsense attitude, this person has little tolerance for frivolity, niceties, or trickery. A direct, honest approach works best.

**Social Skill Check TN Modifiers:** Water +2, Earth –2

**Common Way of Unmasking:** Inappropriate Outburst

##### Shrewd

This individual is used to maneuvering around powerful people, and tends to avoid direct conflict. They are also very wary of such efforts on the part of others.

**Social Skill Check TN Modifiers:** Air +2, Fire –2  
**Common Way of Unmasking:** Panicked Retreat

### Constructing Conflicts

The open-ended nature of character advancement in _Legend of the Five Rings_ makes it difficult to generalize exactly how much of a challenge a given NPC will be as an opponent. A highly trained courtier might be no better at fighting than they were at the time of character creation, because they have focused on intrigue instead of martial arts. Meanwhile, with the same amount of XP, a character from the same school could have invested their experience in becoming knowledgeable, well-spoken, and skilled at arms, and have reached modest proficiency in all spheres without excelling in any.

However, when assessed together, a group’s power is somewhat more predictable based on the PCs’ overall school rank. To give GMs a rough idea of an NPC’s efficacy, each sample NPC profile has a conflict rank for both combat and intrigue, to provide a rough guideline for deciding how many foes to hurl at the PCs.

##### Determining Group Rank

To estimate the **Group Rank** (the rough power of the PCs), take the sum of the group’s school ranks.

##### Determining Encounter Rank

To estimate the **Encounter Rank** (how challenging the NPCs in a conflict scene will be) for a scene, take the sum of the combat [combat] (for battles) or intrigue [intrigue] (for social scenes) conflict ranks of all NPCs opposing the PCs.

##### Gauging an Encounter

Compare the group rank to the encounter rank and consult the following:

- If the group rank is **roughly equal to the encounter rank**, the PCs are generally matched with the force of NPCs. They most likely have a solid chance of prevailing, though they could still lose if they grow overconfident (or fortune forsakes them).
- If the group rank is **1.5 to 2 times the encounter rank**, the PCs have a significant edge over the NPCs, and are very likely to win without significant losses.
- If the group rank is **0.5 times the encounter rank or lower**, the PCs are significantly outmatched—cunning or luck will be needed to take the day, and this might even be a battle best avoided!

### Closed Versus Open Rolls

As the GM, it’s your choice whether you want to make a given roll for an NPC open (in sight of the players) or closed (behind a screen or otherwise obscured). It’s a good idea to make most rolls openly, because of abilities that allow PCs to alter enemy rolls, but this isn’t a hard-and-fast rule.

If a player wishes to use an ability that alters an enemy roll, you can perform the alteration for them on the NPC’s dice pool (in an optimal manner for the player’s desired outcome). If for some reason even this would give away too much information, you can give that character 1 Void point (and they do not count as having used the ability), but this is an option you probably shouldn’t exercise too often.

**COMMONERS**

_There are two broad_

_types of commoners in Rokugan: the heimin, or “half-persons,” who make up the caste of laborers known as the bonge and do much of the fundamental labor required to allow the Empire to function; and the hinin, or so-called “nonpeople,” who perform many of the most base and demeaning jobs._

_Within the category of heimin, there is further stratification. In order of social importance, the first are farmers, then artisans and craftspeople, and finally merchants, who make nothing themselves and only deal in trade and commerce._

_Hinin include criminals, many entertainers,_

_and those considered “untouchables” because they handle dead flesh and refuse. Hinin are considered “nonpeople”_

_and treated as little more than property._

##### Outlaws and Rōnin

Like any society, Rokugan has its share of antisocial types who flout its norms and laws in pursuit of personal gain. While most of these miscreants are simply lawless individuals or members of loose gangs, some have come together in organized bands. While the involvement in such illegal undertakings is dishonorable in the extreme, samurai still can fall prey to greed as well.

Beyond outlaws, there are the samurai known as rōnin, or “wave men,” as they come and go like the waves of the ocean. Rōnin are generally considered disreputable, often being treated with scorn and contempt by clan samurai. Moreover, without a lord, samurai have no access to the things a lord normally provides to vassals, such as food, equipment, and lodging. The life of a rōnin is, therefore, a hard one, as they are forced to fend for themselves as mercenaries and criminals in an Empire often hostile to them.

### Twists of Fate

As the GM, there are times when it is not narratively satisfying for a villain to just die. To avoid this, you can apply a twist of fate—an unpreventable outcome that saves the NPC, but also compensates the players. First, tell the players about the twist of fate and narrate how the NPC escapes. At the end of the session, each PC receives 1 extra XP, as they come to terms with fortune's turns and grow stronger in anticipation of their eventual clash with their foe.

**MUSHA SHUGYŌ**

_With the permission of their daimyō, a clan samurai could undertake a musha shugyō, or “warrior’s pilgrimage.” A samurai on a musha shugyō becomes a rōnin, temporarily casting aside their loyalties and wandering the Empire to learn as much as possible while perfecting their skills as a warrior. It is essential that the erstwhile samurai make no reference to their clan during the pilgrimage. Once the requirements of the musha shugyō are fulfilled, the rōnin returns to their clan as a samurai._

### NPC Squads

Having a large number of NPCs in a conflict can make it chaotic for any GM, especially if each one has an initiative value. To streamline handling a group of NPCs of the same type, treat them as a squad. **A squad consists of two or more NPCs with the same name and profile, and functions as follows:**

**Initiative:** They generate a single initiative

value, and take turns in any order you choose at their initiative value each round.

**Group Actions:** If you want to reduce the

number of rolls the squad makes, simply have all members of the squad but one take the Assist action targeting the one member who performs a different action. Having more than six NPCs in a squad is not advised, simply due to the size of the dice pools involved.

**Group Stances:** All members of a squad  
should assume the same stance each round.  
**Group Strife:** Assign the squad a squad

strife value equal to 3 times an individual member’s composure. This can be helpful as a means to determine the point at which undisciplined foes likely break and run.

Generally, you’ll want to make squads of minion NPCs like ashigaru and bandits, since they’ll be the bulk of unnamed participants in a skirmish, but there’s no mechanical reason you couldn’t have a squad of adversaries— they’d just be more complex to manage and likely more dangerous to the PCs.
